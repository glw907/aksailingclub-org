Sunday was a full day at the club, with hammers swinging and frames coming together. We started at 11:00 in the morning and kept at it until about 6:30 in the evening. By the time we wrapped up, we had three new dock sections ready for service.

Many thanks go to Dave Johnson for organizing and leading the work, and to TL Stanbro, Will Merriman, and Patrick Folds for putting in a long, steady day of building.

### Background: Upgrading the ASC Dock System

For the last several seasons, the club has been gradually moving toward a new standard for dock construction. Our older plywood-decked docks were built in the early 2000s, and while they’ve served us well, they are now at the end of their lifespan. The plywood tops are softening, the frames are showing wear, and maintenance has become a losing battle.

The new design is built to last: treated 4x6 beam framing, industrial-grade metal fittings, commercial floats, and locally recycled plastic decking. We expect each section to give us 20–30 years of service. It’s a big investment of time and materials, but one that should pay off in durability and safety for decades. The long-term goal is to replace every plywood dock with these new builds while also making more efficient use of our limited waterfront space.

### The Day’s Work

We focused on three sections: two 20-foot “main” docks and one 20-foot finger dock. The new main docks will take the place of two aging plywood units, giving us sturdier, longer-lasting walkways. The finger dock is a special upgrade. At 20 feet long, it’s larger than our typical 16-foot fingers and will allow us to park two keelboats side-by-side. It can also be used for dinghy tie-ups, helping us maximize every bit of dock space as our fleet continues to grow.

TL, Dave, Will, Patrick, and I arrived at 11:00 a.m. and started the cutting. From there, we pieced together the heavy beam frames, bolted on the metal hardware, and attached the floats. By evening, we had all three frames assembled.

### Looking Ahead

We still have three more plywood docks to replace: two standard 8x20s and the tricky corner dock by the hoist. That last one is both more complex and slightly newer, so it will probably be the final piece in the upgrade project.

In the meantime, the frames we built will wait on shore until the spring work party, when we’ll add the decking and replace the old section. We did come up short on a few fasteners this time, but Dave is picking those up so the finishing touches can be handled at the fall work party. We also noticed a bit of transport damage to one of the floats, which TL will patch with JB Weld once we have a stretch of dry, sunny weather.
