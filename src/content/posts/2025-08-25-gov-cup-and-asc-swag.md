Ahoy, sailors! The Gov Cup — usually the biggest regatta of the season — is this weekend. And all the details are below in the Sailing Instructions (SIs), which are the event-specific rules that tell you how the regatta will run — things like the schedule, race courses, and how results are scored.

The SIs work alongside the Racing Rules of Sailing to give you all the details you need for race day.

We've traditionally been pretty informal at the ASC, and we intend to keep things that way. But we're also a teaching club, and we want to give sailors the knowledge they need to sail at other clubs, where you'll almost always receive SIs as part of regatta participation.

In that vein, we're going to start providing SIs ahead of all of our regattas. The Governor's Cup SIs are below, and the format is pretty standard, although the ASC SIs provide a little more explanation than you'll usually see to make sure they're maximally accessible to new sailors. We also include a few ASC-specific sections.

Make sure to note the volunteer call for both the race committee and the Styrofoam lake cleanup. And even if you're not racing, don't miss the Saturday evening potluck or the Commodore's Breakfast on Sunday morning. Details on all of the above are in the SIs.

## Sailing Instructions for the 2025 Governor's Cup

**August 30–31, 2025**
**Alaska Sailing Club, Big Lake, Alaska**

**WELCOME:** The Governor's Cup is the granddaddy of all the ASC regattas. The first "Gov Cup" was held in 1966; we haven't missed a year since. This makes the Governor's Cup one of Alaska's longest continuously running sporting events. Our long-standing tradition is to hold the race on Labor Day weekend, rain or shine.

This is usually our best-attended regatta of the year. We try hard to have at least seven races in the series, and we may conduct as many as nine races if the weather allows. The victor gets their name engraved on a large and uniquely ugly pass-around trophy that has circulated since the first race in '66. As always, the Gov Cup includes a potluck after racing on Saturday, the famed Commodore's Breakfast on Sunday morning, and lots of campfire time with all your sailing friends.

ASC regattas are fun and welcoming for all sailors — new or experienced. Don’t have a boat or crew? Come anyway, and we’ll help you get on the water. Experienced members are glad to assist, but if you’d like help rigging, please start working on your boat at least 90 minutes before the skipper’s meeting on the first day.

**RULES:** Racing will be governed by [The Racing Rules of Sailing (RRS)](https://d7qh6ksdplczd.cloudfront.net/sailing/wp-content/uploads/2025/07/29083752/2025-2028-RRS-with-Changes-and-Corrections.pdf) and these Sailing Instructions. The focus is on friendly competition and supporting new sailors.

**ENTRY AND FEES:** There’s no advance registration or entry fee for the Governor’s Cup.

**ELIGIBILITY:** Any member of the Alaska Sailing Club or their guest may participate. Each boat must have at least one ASC member on board. Club boats are available on a first-rigged/first-served basis, and once a skipper claims a club boat, it is theirs for the duration of the regatta.

**RACE COMMITTEE VOLUNTEERS NEEDED:** Christopher Cryan has volunteered to lead the race committee this weekend, but we need a few more able bodies to assist him. No experience is required, and this is a great way to participate if you're new to racing. Christopher is an experienced instructor, so you'll learn a lot by working with him. Interested volunteers can contact Brie Busey, and her contact information is at the end of these SIs.

### Schedule and Check-In

**SCHEDULE:**
**On Day 1 (Saturday, August 30th)**, the skipper’s meeting will be held at 1:00 p.m. All boats must be fully rigged and ready to sail before the skipper’s meeting begins. Check-in happens at the skipper’s meeting.

The first warning signal will be no earlier than 30 minutes after the skipper’s meeting, which allows time to launch and sail to the starting area. The race committee may delay the start further if wind conditions are not suitable for racing.

**On Day 2 (Sunday, August 31st)**, the first warning signal will be at 11:00 a.m.

The Governor's Cup will have a target of nine races. No more than five races will be sailed in a single day. The actual number of races sailed will depend on wind and weather conditions. No races will be started after 5:00 p.m. on the final day of racing.

We strongly recommend arriving early enough to allow time for warm-up sailing before the races. Taking some time to practice (tacks, jibes, spinnaker hoists and douses, upwind sailing, etc.) without the pressure of a race will help you sail more effectively and happily during the regatta. If possible, arriving the evening before is even better, as winds at that time are often the most consistent.

**SOCIAL EVENTS:** We'll have our traditional post-regatta dinner potluck after racing on Saturday. (This may be late depending on the wind, so bring snacks while you race.) We'll also have the famed Commodore's Breakfast on Sunday morning at 9:00 AM. The Black and Ramirez families will be hosting, so you know it'll be epic!

**LAKE CLEANUP:** In keeping with our longstanding tradition of caring for the lake that we all love so much, Elyane Hunter will be leading a crew to remove some large styrofoam floats that are stranded by the club. We definitely need some volunteers for this one, and we'll attempt to work this project into the inevitable low-wind parts of the weekend.

**PHOTO SHARING:** We always encourage sailors to post event photos and stories in the `#general` or `#racing` channels on the [ASC Discord server](/members/discord-server/) to celebrate the regatta and share the fun with fellow sailors.

Everyone — racers, recreational sailors, friends, and family — is welcome to attend.

### Fleets and Start Order

**FLEETS:** We have two planned fleets for the Governor's Cup:

- **Portsmouth fleet:** Open to all boat types, scored using the [Portsmouth handicap system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portsmouth_Yardstick). This fleet is relaxed, beginner-friendly, and a great place for new sailors to start.

- **Buccaneer 18 fleet:** One-design fleet for Buccaneer 18s, generally more competitive but still welcoming. The "Bucc fleet" is more competitive than Portsmouth, but it remains welcoming to newcomers who want to learn.

**Choosing a fleet: **Buccaneer sailors may race in either fleet. Buccs racing in Portsmouth must register as *spinnaker* (rating 87) or *non-spinnaker* (rating 87.87). Bucc sailors must declare spinnaker or non-spinnaker at the skipper’s meeting and use the same configuration for the entire regatta.

**FLEET STARTING ORDER:** On Day 1, the Buccaneer 18 fleet will start first. For the rest of the regatta, starts will alternate between fleets where possible. The race committee may adjust the order based on wind and weather conditions.

### Race Area and Courses

**RACING AREA:** Big Lake in the vicinity of ASC.

**COURSES:** The race committee will set either a Windward (W) or a Windward/Leeward (WL) course.

- A **Windward course** consists of a start, a single windward leg, and then a finish downwind.

- A **Windward/Leeward course** consists of a start, an upwind leg to the windward mark, a downwind leg to the leeward mark set past the start/finish line, and then a final upwind leg to the finish line. This means you start upwind, sail downwind, then finish upwind.

- If the course is designated with a “2” (W2 or WL2), boats will sail the course twice around before finishing.

- Unless the race committee signals otherwise, round all marks to port.

The course to be sailed will be posted on the race committee boat before each start. The race committee may also announce the course by VHF radio on channel 68.

**MARKS:** Orange, yellow, or red inflatable marks will be used to mark the course.

### Starts and Finishes

**LINES AND FINISHES:** The start/finish line is between a red mark and the race committee boat. Starts and finishes use this same line. To finish, cross the line from the course side (from the last mark toward the line).

**STARTING SEQUENCE:** The start of each race will use the standard *5-4-1-0 minute sequence* described in Rule 26 of the Racing Rules of Sailing. Often, the race committee will sound a few short courtesy horn blasts about six minutes before the start to get everyone’s attention and signal that the sequence is about to begin.

At five minutes before the start, the class flag for your fleet will be raised. Four minutes before the start, the preparatory flag will be raised. At one minute before the start, the preparatory flag will be lowered. Finally, at the starting signal (zero), the class flag will be lowered, and the race begins.

Watch the flags and listen for the horns — together they give you a clear countdown to the start. If it isn’t your fleet’s turn to start, please stay well away from the starting line area. This means don’t sail across or hover near the line while another fleet is in their starting sequence. Keeping the line clear helps the other sailors start fairly and makes things less confusing for everyone.

**FLAGS USED DURING THE STARTS:**

- *Class flag for the Buccaneer 18 fleet:* White with two black crossed sabers. Hoisted 5 minutes before the start, lowered at the start.

- *Class flag for the Portsmouth fleet:* White with two black crossed sabers. Hoisted 5 minutes before the start, lowered at the start.

- *Preparatory flag:* Blue with a white square in the center. Hoisted 4 minutes before the start, lowered 1 minute before the start.

**OTHER COMMON FLAGS:**

- *Postponement (AP):* Red/white vertical stripes. Hoisted when racing is postponed, lowered with one sound one minute before the new warning signal.

- *General recall:* Yellow triangle on blue. Hoisted when all boats in a start must restart, lowered with one sound at the new warning signal.

- *Individual recall (X flag):* White with blue cross. Hoisted when one or more boats are over early, lowered when those boats have returned or after 4 minutes, whichever is sooner.

**OVER EARLY PROCEDURE:** If a boat is over the line at the start, the race committee will raise the X flag and sound one horn. The race committee will attempt to notify the boat by flag, radio, and voice from the committee boat. However, it is always the sailor’s responsibility to know whether they are over early and to return completely to the pre-start side of the line by recrossing the line or by sailing around an end, then start.

### Penalties and Time Limits

**PENALTIES:**

- If a boat touches a mark, she must complete a One-Turn Penalty (one tack and one jibe).

- If a boat breaks a rule when meeting another boat (Part 2 of the RRS), she must complete a Two-Turns Penalty (two tacks and two jibes).

- Penalties should be taken as soon as possible after the incident, in clear water and away from other boats, so racing is not disrupted.

- If you’re unsure whether you broke a rule, it’s usually better to take the penalty right away and keep racing rather than risk disqualification.

**TIME LIMITS:**

- A boat must round the first mark within 30 minutes of her start. Boats failing to do so will be scored DNF (Did Not Finish).

- The time limit for each race is 60 minutes. If no boat finishes within 60 minutes, the race will be abandoned.

- After the first boat in your fleet finishes, all remaining boats have 15 minutes to finish. Boats still racing after that time will be scored TLE (Time Limit Expired), but will receive a score one place higher than the last boat that finished.

### Scoring and Race Results

**SCORING:** Scoring will use the *Low Point System* from Appendix A of the Racing Rules of Sailing (RRS). In this system, first place earns 1 point, second place earns 2 points, third place earns 3 points, and so on. At the end of the regatta, the boat with the lowest total score wins. The Governor's Cup will have a target of nine races.

To account for the occasional tough race, sailors may be allowed to “throw out” (ignore) their worst result. If fewer than five races are completed, all races count. If five to eight races are completed, each sailor may discard their worst score. If nine to twelve races are completed, each sailor may discard their two worst scores.

Boats that do not start (DNS), do not finish (DNF), or are disqualified (DSQ) will score points equal to the number of boats that started the race plus one.

If two or more boats are tied on points at the end of the regatta, the tie is broken by comparing their race finishes one by one. For example, if Boat A has a first place and Boat B’s best finish is second, Boat A wins the tie. If their best finishes are the same, the tie is decided by the next best finish, and so on. If they are still tied after that, the boat that finished ahead in the last race sailed will win the tie.

*Example:* In a regatta with six races, a sailor with finishes of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, and 6 could discard their worst result (the 6). Their final score would be 1+2+3+4+5 = 15 points.

**RACE RESULTS:** Preliminary results will be posted in the `#racing` channel on the [ASC Discord server](/members/discord-server/). These are provisional and may change pending protests or scoring corrections. Results discussions are a great chance to learn how scoring works, so feel free to ask questions!

Final results will be posted on the ASC website.

### Protests and Conduct

**PROTESTS:** On-the-water resolution is encouraged whenever possible. If an incident cannot be resolved on the water, the race committee will meet after the day’s racing to hear the protest and make a ruling. Other experienced sailors may be invited to participate in the discussion, but the race committee will make the final decision. Protest discussions are intended to be a friendly learning opportunity for all sailors.

**RACE CONDUCT:** Keep it friendly! Help newcomers, encourage sportsmanship, and remember we’re here to have fun and grow our sailing community.

### Safety and Operations

**SAFETY:** Life jackets are required for all participants per ASC rules. For cold-season events, wetsuits or dry suits are recommended. All sailors are responsible for deciding whether conditions are safe for them to sail. Safety is always the top priority. The race committee may abandon races at any time if conditions are unsafe.

**COMMUNICATIONS:** The race committee will monitor channel 68 and may provide announcements.

**CLUB BOAT CARE AND STORAGE:**

- While club boats are docked, sails should be lowered. For club Lasers, the outhaul should be fully uncleated so the sail flies free of the boom. This prevents wind gusts from damaging the boats.

- Boats should be properly secured while at the dock, including a bow line, a stern line, and a spring line. The trailing end of the stern line may be used as a spring. On club Lidos, the spring can run through the jib cleat. For Buccaneers, Skipjacks, and the Catalina, tie the end of the line to the shroud with a bowline. Attach a fender where the boat might touch the dock. If you are unsure, ask an experienced sailor for help.

- Club Lasers may be left on the green small-boat launch dock during the day, but must be removed overnight. For overnight storage, leave them on the grassy area by the small boat rack, with the sail wrapped around the mast and the mast unstepped and laid on the boat.

- The club Buccaneer 18 (“Waldo”) must be removed from the water, drained, and stored on its trailer overnight.

- All sailors are responsible for putting away any boat they use for the regatta, even if they do not stay for both days of racing.

**WHAT TO BRING:**

- *Watch with a countdown timer:* Essential for timing race starts accurately.

- *Drinking water:* Staying hydrated is important, even on cooler days.

- *Snacks:* Quick energy helps you stay sharp during multiple races.

- *A warm, windproof layer:* Weather on Big Lake can change quickly, and being prepared keeps sailing safe and enjoyable.

- *Sunscreen:* Sunlight reflects strongly off the water, so protection helps prevent sunburn even on cloudy days.

A handheld VHF radio is required to race in the Buccaneer 18 fleet. And although it's not required to race in Portsmouth, we still recommend bringing a radio since it helps to hear race committee announcements and safety information.
